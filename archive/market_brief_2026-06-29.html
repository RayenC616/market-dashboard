Global Markets Brief

Session Review — Monday, June 29, 2026  |  Prepared June 30, 2026  |  Institutional Distribution

Executive Summary

U.S. equities staged a sharp risk-on rally Monday: the S&P 500 climbed 1.18% to 7,440.43, the Nasdaq Composite jumped 2.07% to 25,820.14, and the Dow Jones Industrial Average rose 0.59% to a record 52,182.74 — its first close above 52,000 — coinciding with Alphabet's addition to the index. The advance reverses last week's AI-chip-led drawdown, with Communication Services (+3.49%) and Consumer Cyclical (+2.35%) leading sector gains while Basic Materials (-1.71%) and Real Estate (-0.95%) lagged. Asia was broadly firmer, with the Nikkei 225 up 1.26% to 70,516.50 and the Hang Seng up 1.57% to 23,026.68, while the KOSPI slipped 0.20% to 8,394.65; the STOXX 600 was little changed (+0.04% to 636.11). On a trailing basis the dispersion across regions is stark: Korea's KOSPI is up 99.2% year-to-date and Japan's Nikkei 225 is up 40.1% YTD, while Hong Kong's Hang Seng has round-tripped to -10.2% YTD after a sharp pullback over the past month (-8.6%) and week (-3.1%). Commodities were broadly weaker outside energy: gold fell 1.17% to $4,029.40/oz, platinum dropped 1.80%, aluminium fell 3.10% and nickel fell 2.91%, while WTI rose 0.54% to $70.14/bbl after the U.S. and Iran agreed to halt mutual attacks ahead of renewed peace talks. The macro backdrop remains a focal risk: May core PCE held at 3.4% y/y and headline PCE accelerated to 4.1% y/y — the fastest pace since April 2023 — keeping the Fed's policy path in sharp focus ahead of Thursday's June payrolls report (consensus +172k).

  • Communication Services (+3.49%) and Technology (+1.50%) led as the Nasdaq's 2.07% advance reflected a rebound in mega-cap names after Alphabet's inclusion in the Dow validated continued institutional appetite for AI-exposed large caps, reversing part of the prior week's chip-sector drawdown.
  • Consumer Cyclical (+2.35%) and Industrials (+1.61%) outperformed as risk sentiment improved broadly with the S&P 500's 1.18% gain, consistent with a rotation back into cyclicals once the U.S.-Iran de-escalation reduced near-term tail risk to oil-sensitive input costs.
  • Basic Materials (-1.71%) was the weakest sector, consistent with a broad industrial- and precious-metals drawdown: gold fell 1.17% to $4,029.40/oz, platinum fell 1.80% to $1,597.45/oz, aluminium fell 3.10% to $3,099.80/t and nickel fell 2.91% to $16,306.63/t, while European industrial-metal-exposed names (Infineon -4.5%, STMicroelectronics -4.5% in the prior session) were still digesting memory-price concerns flagged after SK Hynix (-8.4%) and Samsung Electronics (-5.3%) fell on memory-demand worries.
  • Real Estate (-0.95%) and Utilities (-0.73%) lagged, the classic pattern when rate-hike expectations firm: the May core PCE print holding at 3.4% y/y reinforced the case for the Fed to stay restrictive, pressuring rate-sensitive, high-duration equity sectors even as the broader tape rallied.

Cross-Cutting Issues & Items Requiring Attention

1. Geopolitical risk premium in oil remains fluid. WTI's rebound to $69.70/bbl followed a U.S.-Iran agreement to halt mutual attacks ahead of fresh peace talks, but a separate round of U.S.-Iran negotiations in Bürgenstock was abruptly postponed and VP Vance's travel was scrapped over "unresolved logistical issues." Tanker flows through the Strait of Hormuz have continued (12mn+ barrels transited overnight without incident), but the on/off cadence of talks keeps energy-sector volatility elevated.
2. Memory-chip demand concerns are a live cross-regional theme. SK Hynix (-8.4%) and Samsung Electronics (-5.3%) sold off sharply in the prior session on memory-demand worries, with the contagion reaching European suppliers (Infineon, STMicroelectronics, both -4.5%). Monday's Nasdaq/Technology rebound (+2.07% / +1.50%) suggests this was treated as a short-term overreaction rather than a structural reset, but the divergence between U.S. mega-cap AI names and Asian memory suppliers bears monitoring into upcoming earnings.
3. Inflation persistence is reasserting itself as the dominant macro risk. Core PCE at 3.4% y/y (highest since October 2023) and headline PCE at 4.1% y/y (highest since April 2023) both point to inflation re-accelerating rather than continuing to converge toward target, which is the key reason gold fell even as geopolitical risk stayed elevated — investors are pricing a Fed that holds rates higher for longer. The June payrolls print on July 2 (consensus +172k) is the next test of whether labor-market resilience corroborates a "higher for longer" stance.
