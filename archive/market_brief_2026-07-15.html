The July 15 session was macro-intensive, with three significant data prints and central bank commentary shaping cross-asset direction.

US CPI — June 2026 (Released July 14, 2026): Headline CPI fell 0.4% MoM (vs. +0.1% expected, prior +0.5%) — the largest monthly decline since April 2020 — driven by a 5.7% drop in energy and a 9.7% plunge in gasoline following partial de-escalation of the US–Iran Hormuz standoff. YoY headline CPI eased to 3.5% (vs. 3.8% estimate, prior 4.2%). Core CPI (ex-food & energy) also surprised to the downside: flat MoM, bringing YoY core to 2.6% (vs. 2.8% estimate, prior 2.9%). Both figures were sourced by BLS and covered by CNBC (July 14, 2026). The report triggered a 14-basis-point intraday decline in the 2-year Treasury yield to 4.14% and prompted traders to scale back CME FedWatch probabilities for a July rate hike.

US PPI — June 2026 (Released July 15, 2026): The Producer Price Index for final demand fell 0.3% MoM (vs. 0.0% consensus), the largest monthly decline in wholesale prices since April 2025. Annually, PPI rose 5.5% (vs. 6.2% forecast, prior month revised sharply down to 6.0% from 6.6%). Core PPI (ex-food & energy) rose 0.2% MoM vs. 0.3% expected. Core PPI less trade services +0.1% MoM, up 5.1% YoY. The BLS data was corroborated by Investing.com and Reuters. Fed Governor Williams stated "inflation has peaked and should edge down," while Fed Chair Kevin Warsh testified before the Senate Banking Committee that rate hikes remain an option, though not necessarily imminent; traders priced in ~60% probability of a quarter- to half-point increase by the October meeting.

China GDP — Q2 2026 (Released July 15, 2026): China's economy expanded 4.3% YoY in Q2 (vs. 4.5% consensus, prior Q1: 5.0%), the weakest quarterly reading since 2022. Fixed-asset investment and retail sales remained subdued; industrial production relatively resilient; urban unemployment edged down to 5%. The data strengthened the case for additional fiscal stimulus ahead of the Politburo meeting later in July. Mainland CSI 300 fell 0.2% on the miss while Hang Seng gained 1.4%, supported by healthcare and real estate sectors.

Bank of Canada — July 2026: The BoC held its policy rate at 2.25%, as universally expected. Governor communications emphasized that growth is expected to pick up and inflation is projected to ease toward 2%, though uncertainty remains elevated. (Source: Bank of Canada press release)

No major US employment or GDP data was released on July 15. The next US data landmarks: Empire State Manufacturing Index came in at 5.7 for July (in line); weekly EIA crude inventories reported a drawdown consistent with seasonal demand. The next CPI release is scheduled for August 12, 2026.

Cross-Cutting Attention Items

1. Iran–Strait of Hormuz Geopolitical Escalation
Fresh US airstrikes near the Strait of Hormuz were confirmed by CENTCOM on July 15, hours after Trump reinstated the shipping blockade on Iran. The US simultaneously abandoned plans for a 20% cargo tariff on Hormuz transit (rescinded after Gulf ally pressure), but Houthi ballistic missile and drone attacks on Saudi Arabia intensified. WTI rose 8.27% over the past week to $79.60/bbl. The 12-month YTD gain in crude is +38.6%. Institutional portfolios with long energy exposure benefit near-term, but a full Strait closure (~20% of global seaborne oil) remains the tail risk to monitor. Sources: CNBC (July 15), Saxo Bank Quick Take.
2. IBM Earnings Disaster: Enterprise Software Demand Warning
IBM's record single-day decline of 25.2% on July 15 sent shockwaves across global enterprise tech and European IT services. Capgemini and Dassault Systèmes fell materially in sympathy; Ericsson also plunged nearly 13% after warning that higher costs would pressure network margins. IBM's miss — preliminary Q2 revenues and earnings below consensus on weaker software and infrastructure — raises the question of whether AI adoption is cannibalizing traditional enterprise software spend or merely being deferred. With ERP/SaaS multiples still elevated, investors should reassess enterprise technology exposure ahead of Salesforce, Oracle, and SAP results. Sources: CNBC, Saxo Bank.
3. China Growth Slowdown: Commodities and Emerging Market Implications
China's Q2 GDP of 4.3% missed the 4.5% consensus and is the weakest quarterly growth in three years. The shortfall was broad-based — weak private consumption, sluggish real estate investment, and muted fixed-asset investment. While industrial production and exports held up, the structural drag from the property sector is not resolved. Commodity-sensitive portfolios face downside risk (Copper -0.58%, Silver -2.83% on the session). The Politburo meeting later in July could announce additional fiscal measures; watch for PBoC reserve requirement cuts or large-scale infrastructure bond issuance. Australia, Brazil, and other commodity-exporting EM currencies remain exposed. Source: Saxo Bank, CNBC.
4. Stripe/Advent–PayPal M&A Offer: Fintech Consolidation Signal
Reuters reported on July 15 that Stripe and Advent International submitted a $53 billion acquisition offer for PayPal ($60.50/share), sending PYPL +19% and driving the largest single-day gain in the Consumer Cyclical sector. If completed, this would rank among the largest fintech M&A transactions on record and could trigger further consolidation across payments, digital wallets, and buy-now-pay-later platforms. Regulatory scrutiny under the current DOJ/FTC posture is uncertain. Investors in Square (Block), Adyen, and Worldpay should assess deal probability, valuation comps, and the precedent for sector premia. Source: Reuters (July 15, 2026).
