Executive Summary

U.S. equities posted a broad advance on Monday, July 6, 2026, with the S&P 500 gaining 0.72% to close at 7,537.43 and the Nasdaq Composite jumping 1.12% to 26,121.16 — the latter's strongest session in three weeks. The Dow Jones Industrial Average climbed 155.84 points (+0.29%) to 53,055.91, clearing the 53,000 mark for the first time on record. The session's tone was unambiguously risk-on: chip stocks rebounded sharply after a late-June rout following Foxconn's stronger-than-expected Q2 sales print (a proxy for sustained AI infrastructure demand), while Big Tech names (Alphabet, Apple, Meta, Tesla) added to gains. In cross-asset terms, gold rose 1.03% to $4,155.10/oz and silver gained 2.11% — both bid as insurance against lingering Middle East uncertainty — while WTI crude slipped 0.20% to $68.55/bbl after OPEC+ raised output targets and the Strait of Hormuz remained open. The macro backdrop was light on scheduled releases; investors awaited Wednesday's FOMC minutes from Chair Kevin Warsh's inaugural June 16–17 meeting, which will be parsed closely for rate-path guidance.

June 2026 Nonfarm Payrolls (released Thursday, July 2, 2026):

The U.S. economy added only 57,000 nonfarm payroll jobs in June 2026 — sharply below the consensus estimate of approximately 110,000–115,000 and well below the downwardly revised 129,000 added in May. This was the weakest monthly gain in four months. Almost all gains accrued in healthcare; notable sectors (retail, leisure/hospitality) added zero jobs despite the World Cup and U.S. 250th anniversary celebrations. Average hourly earnings rose 3.5% YoY (June), and the unemployment rate ticked down to 4.2% from 4.3%, reflecting labor-force contraction rather than hiring strength. The U.S. has added 552,000 jobs in the first half of 2026 — roughly five times all of 2025's net job gains, per Keel Point. Markets on July 6 digested this print as broadly labor-market-cooling but not alarm-inducing, given the unemployment rate stability. Cross-Cutting Issues & Attention Items

1. FOMC Minutes (Wednesday, July 8) — Rate Path Inflection Risk

The June 16–17 FOMC minutes, due Wednesday, represent the single most significant near-term market catalyst for the week. Chair Kevin Warsh's inaugural meeting occurred against a backdrop of core PCE at 3.4% (May 2026) and a rapidly cooling labor market (June payrolls: 57,000 vs. 110K consensus). If the minutes reveal internal debate about pre-emptive easing — despite inflation remaining above target — expect a rally in rate-sensitive sectors (REITs, Utilities, smaller caps) and a steepening of the yield curve. Conversely, uniformly hawkish language would pressure tech valuations on a higher-for-longer read. The 10-year at 4.50% is a key technical level. [Source: CNBC, https://www.cnbc.com/2026/07/06/us-treasury-yields-investors-look-ahead-to-fomc-meeting-minutesu.html; Federal Reserve, https://www.federalreserve.gov/monetarypolicy/fomccalendars.htm]

2. SpaceX (SPCX) Nasdaq-100 Inclusion — Passive Flow, AI Valuation Reset

SpaceX joined the Nasdaq-100 effective Tuesday, July 7, 2026 — triggering mandatory passive rebalancing by ETFs and mutual funds tracking the index (primarily QQQ, with >$330 billion AUM). SpaceX has been among the most volatile large-cap additions in recent memory, having surged ~22% on its IPO (June 11) and subsequently fallen 16.4% in a single session (June 22). Its valuation — targeting ~$3 trillion — sets an implicit floor/ceiling for broader AI infrastructure expectations. The rebalancing-driven buying wave may create index-level distortions in the near term, particularly in communication services and tech hardware names that are partially displaced. 3. Middle East / Strait of Hormuz — Re-escalation Risk

The Strait of Hormuz, which the U.S. and Iran partially normalized through a June 17 peace framework, saw renewed friction Monday: UKMTO reported an oil tanker struck by an "unknown projectile" off Oman, and Iran was reported to have resumed isolated attacks. OPEC+ agreed to raise output targets as flows normalized, holding WTI near $68/bbl — still $15–20 below pre-crisis levels. Any sustained re-closure or escalation would immediately reverse the deflationary impulse that commodity markets have absorbed since mid-June, reigniting stagflation risk. Shell's Q2 guidance (integrated gas production down 33% QoQ due to conflict) underscores the supply impairment that persists even in a nominally open strait. 4. NATO Ankara Summit / European Defense Spending — Sector Rotation Catalyst

The NATO summit in Ankara (July 7–8) is exposing a transatlantic defense-spending divide that has direct investable implications. NATO is showcasing billion-dollar arms procurement contracts to satisfy President Trump's demand that allies contribute 5% of GDP to defense. The Saab GlobalEye aircraft deal (+5.2% Saab shares) and TKMS submarine contract (Hanwha Ocean -23%) illustrate the winner/loser dynamics. European defense equities (Rheinmetall, Leonardo, BAE Systems, Saab) have been the year's strongest sector globally; further spending commitments at the summit would extend that trend. Simultaneously, Trump's stated posture — "We're not going away, we're just doing less" on European conventional defense — introduces a medium-term NATO cohesion risk that asset managers should price.
