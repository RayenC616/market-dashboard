Session Review — Tuesday, July 7, 2026  |  Prepared July 8, 2026, 07:00 KST  |  Institutional Distribution

Executive Summary

U.S. equities retreated Tuesday as a fresh leg of the semiconductor sell-off overshadowed an otherwise quiet macro calendar: the S&P 500 fell 0.45% to 7,503.85 and the Nasdaq Composite dropped 1.16% to 25,818.69, with Intel (-9.66%), Teradyne (-9.59%) and Applied Materials (-6.46%) among the hardest hit after Samsung Electronics' blowout preliminary Q2 profit report (+19x y/y) triggered profit-taking rather than relief, compounded by reports that China's DeepSeek is developing its own in-house AI chip. The damage was sharpest in Asia, where Samsung and SK Hynix's outsized index weight dragged the KOSPI down 4.91% on the day and 9.68% over the week; the Nikkei 225 fell 2.12% and Hong Kong's Hang Seng slipped a comparatively modest 0.51%, while Europe's STOXX 600 eased 0.65%. Elsewhere, WTI crude jumped 2.76% to $70.44/bbl after a projectile strike on an LNG carrier near the Strait of Hormuz revived concerns about the durability of the existing U.S.-Iran de-escalation, and gold and silver extended their weekly gains (+3.06%/+4.74%) as investors trimmed Fed rate-hike bets following a soft June payrolls report (+57K vs. +115K consensus). Not every stock moved with the tape: Cognizant (+6.21%), Occidental Petroleum (+5.88%) and Cboe Global Markets (+5.53%) posted the S&P 500's best single-day gains, each on its own distinct catalyst detailed below.

Market Drivers

1. Global semiconductor/AI-chip valuation reset. Samsung Electronics' preliminary Q2 operating profit of roughly $58B (+19x y/y, ahead of estimates) nonetheless triggered a sell-the-news reaction — Samsung shares fell as much as 10% intraday in Seoul on profit-taking after a 150% run this year, with Deutsche Bank noting the beat was "only" 6% ahead of consensus. Reports that China's DeepSeek is developing its own in-house AI chip added a fresh competitive/demand-durability worry on top. The impact was concentrated but severe: Intel (-9.66%) and Teradyne (-9.59%) led S&P 500 decliners, Applied Materials fell roughly 6.5%, and per reporting at least 25 semiconductor names are down 20%+ since June 25. Because KOSPI is heavily weighted toward Samsung and SK Hynix, Korea absorbed the sharpest index-level hit (-4.91% on the day, -9.68% on the week), with Japan's chip-equipment exposure pulling the Nikkei down 2.12%. This reads as a valuation check rather than a fundamental break — stretched AI-chip multiples mean even good news can trigger a sharp pullback.
2. Strait of Hormuz shipping attack lifts oil's geopolitical risk premium. A projectile strike on a fully laden LNG carrier owned by a Qatar state shipping company, near the Omani coast while exiting the Strait of Hormuz, renewed concern among shipowners and raised questions over the durability of the existing U.S.-Iran agreement to halt mutual attacks. WTI rose 2.76% to $70.44/bbl on the news, even though crude remains near its lowest level since late February as growing OPEC+ supply expectations continue to weigh on the broader market — illustrating how a single incident can move price at the margin without resetting the underlying supply/demand backdrop. The same risk-off, geopolitically-charged tape helped lift energy-sector equities: Occidental Petroleum (+5.88%) was among the S&P 500's best performers.
3. Weak June payrolls reshape the Fed's rate path. Nonfarm payrolls rose just 57,000 in June, well below the 115,000 consensus and the downwardly-revised 129,000 added in May; the unemployment rate ticked down to 4.2%, but only because of a falling labor-force participation rate rather than underlying strength. Following the report, CME FedWatch data showed the odds of a July hike falling from 29% to 22%, with the odds of a September hold rising from 35.8% to 46.8%. With headline/core PCE inflation still running hot (4.1%/3.4% y/y as of the most recent read), the data reinforces a "too weak to hike, too hot to cut" backdrop that keeps the Fed on hold and rate-sensitive assets volatile into the next data prints.
4. Precious metals catch a safe-haven and rates-driven bid. Gold and silver extended weekly gains (+3.06% and +4.74% respectively) as the Hormuz-linked geopolitical risk premium combined with the paring-back of Fed rate-hike bets — lower real-rate expectations reduce the opportunity cost of holding non-yielding bullion. Reporting points to gold's first weekly gain since late May, a reversal after both metals had given back some of their historic 2025 rally (gold +66%, silver +135%) earlier in 2026. Silver's move is also getting an assist from industrial demand layered on top of investment flows.
